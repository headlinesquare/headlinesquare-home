## 1. Trump Administration Domestic Agenda

### 1.1. The "Big, Beautiful Bill": Fiscal and Social Impacts

The administration's centerpiece legislation, referred to as the "Big, Beautiful Bill," is poised to enact sweeping fiscal and social policy changes affecting tens of millions of Americans. A core component of the bill is a dramatic reallocation of federal funds. One analysis reports the legislation will cut Medicaid by an estimated $1 trillion while concurrently increasing the budget for Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) by $75 billion [1]. The bill introduces stringent new work requirements for Medicaid recipients, a provision raising alarms for non-traditional caregivers, such as a mother providing full-time care for her disabled adult son who may lose benefits if she cannot prove outside employment [2]. While the bill offers some tax adjustments for seniors on Social Security, it does not, as some may have hoped, eliminate taxes on those benefits entirely [3]. The legislation is also creating significant disruption in other sectors. Healthcare experts warn its provisions could precipitate the shutdown of an entire category of hospitals, impacting multitudes of patients [4], while students in medical school are expressing deep anxiety over a new, restrictive cap on student loans they say threatens their ability to complete their education [5]. The bill's extensive reach also includes provisions that will shape the nation's transportation future [6].

### 1.2. Governance, Regulation, and Federal Power

The administration is moving to reshape regulatory bodies and assert federal authority. Multiple outlets report President Trump has stated he "may take over governance of Washington, DC," though the provided headlines do not specify the legal or administrative mechanisms for such a potential action [1]. In a significant regulatory shift, the White House is reportedly seeking to eliminate the Chemical Safety Board, an independent agency that investigates industrial chemical accidents. Notably, this move is opposed by chemical manufacturers themselves [2]. In public health, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) has formally ended its emergency response to the H5N1 bird flu [3]. And in a widely-felt change to travel policy, the Transportation Security Administration (TSA) has announced it will end its "shoes-off" policy for airport security, reversing a procedure that had been in place since 2006 [4].
