## 1. Economic and Trade Policy

### 1.1. Administration Pushes Aggressive Tariff Strategy

The Trump administration is advancing a significant protectionist trade strategy centered on a unilateral 100% tariff for all computer chips manufactured outside the United States [1]. This policy, which proponents frame as a tool to compel domestic manufacturing investment, is widely expected to increase the cost of consumer electronics [2]. Reports suggest a potential carve-out may exist, with a possible 0% tariff for major U.S. investors like Apple, creating an incentive structure for specific corporations [3]. This action is part of a broader, aggressive tariff push, as evidenced by Switzerland facing a new 39% U.S. tariff after diplomatic talks with its president failed in Washington [4]. While some analysis indicates growing GOP tolerance for these measures as a political tool, other reports suggest the tariffs remain "unpopular as ever" and may lead to significant economic "pain" for the United States, with a particular impact on the supply chains for American carmakers [5].

## 4. Immigration and Border Security

### 4.1. Plans for Escalated Immigration Enforcement Emerge

The administration's immigration enforcement strategy is reportedly set to escalate dramatically. A leaked memo, attributed to the brother of Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, calls for the U.S. army to assist in nationwide deportation efforts on a long-term basis, stating the military's help will be needed "for years to come" [1]. To meet the staffing needs for such large-scale operations, the Department of Homeland Security has removed all age limits for ICE recruits, widening the pool of potential hires [2]. Reports also suggest the administration's ideological policy may be moving toward a "next stage" of denaturalizing U.S. citizens as a precursor to deportation, a legally contentious proposition [3]. These aggressive policies have immediate international consequences, with reports of deportees being left in "limbo" in Panama, unable to be returned to their original home countries [4].
