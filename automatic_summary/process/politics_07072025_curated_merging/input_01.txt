### 2.1 Texas Flooding: Disaster, Response, and Political Ramifications

A catastrophic flash flood event in central Texas has resulted in a significant and rising death toll. Initial reports of 79 fatalities have now been updated to at least 89, including 28 children, with dozens of people still unaccounted for [1, 2, 3]. The federal response is under severe scrutiny, with critics and a retired NOAA meteorologist directly linking the disaster's severity to recent budget and staff cuts at critical agencies, including the National Weather Service (NWS), NOAA, and FEMA [3, 4]. The NWS has defended its issuance of flood warnings amidst this scrutiny [5]. Preparedness failures at the state and local levels are also being highlighted; reports indicate Texas lawmakers failed earlier this year to pass a bill intended to improve local flood warning systems [6]. In the disaster's aftermath, scientists are reportedly using the event to highlight broader climate risks, framing the flooding as an example of what to expect as the planet warms [7]. Federal officials including Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Senator Ted Cruz have publicly defended the government's handling of the crisis [2]. The disaster has also prompted an international response, with firefighters from Mexico joining relief efforts.
