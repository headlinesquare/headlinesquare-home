## 1. International Relations & Trade Policy

### 1.1. Multi-Front Trade Disputes and Tariff Escalation
The administration is actively escalating trade conflicts on several fronts, deploying tariffs as a primary tool of foreign policy. New actions include the imposition of a 40% tariff on Brazil, a punitive measure accompanied by sanctions against a Brazilian judge involved in the trial of former President Bolsonaro [1]. For Mexico, the administration has extended the 25% tariffs for 90 days as talks continue [2], announcing a formal negotiating period while the tariffs remain in place [3]. A separate and highly acute diplomatic friction has emerged with Canada. Following a move by Prime Minister Mark Carney’s government to recognize a Palestinian state, President Trump has publicly stated this act is a "stumbling block" that makes reaching any trade deal "very hard" [4, 5]. These specific actions unfold as a significant global tariff deadline approaches, drawing focus to the overall status of U.S. trade agreements [6]. U.S. liquor producers are reportedly caught in the disputes and are advocating for the tariffs to be removed [7].

### 1.2. U.S. Response to Israel-Palestine Conflict and Domestic Political Shifts
The United States is intensifying its diplomatic actions regarding the Israel-Palestine conflict, imposing new sanctions on officials from the Palestinian Authority and members of the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) [1]. This occurs as a U.S. envoy dispatched by President Trump has reportedly arrived in Israel. His arrival coincides with reports of a "rising Gaza death toll of Palestinians seeking aid" [2]. These international developments are mirrored by notable shifts in domestic political sentiment. Polling data suggests U.S. support for Israel's actions in Gaza is decreasing. This trend includes growing opposition among Democratic voters and, significantly, an emergent split within the "MAGA movement" [3]. The shifting dynamics were visible in the Senate, where a majority of Democratic senators voted to block certain arms sales to Israel, an event that prompted Senator Bernie Sanders to state, "'The Tide Is Turning'" [4].

### 2.2. Critical Legal Challenge to Tariff Authority
The administration's entire tariff-based trade strategy is facing a critical legal test that could have sweeping consequences. An appeals court in Washington D.C. is scheduled to hear arguments challenging the fundamental legality of the President's use of so-called "reciprocal" tariffs [1]. The case is being closely watched, with some external legal analysis suggesting that a ruling against the administration could "kneecap most Trump tariffs" [2] and put the broader trade agenda "on the line" [3]. This fundamental legal challenge comes to a head just before a stated August 1 deadline for the tariffs to take effect [4]. In a separate development affecting the legal profession, reports suggest that what is being characterized as the President's "war on Big Law" is causing major firms to retreat from providing "pro bono" work for under-resourced clients, referred to as "underdogs" in one headline [5].

Concurrently, a significant policy shift appears to be underway for 'Dreamers.' The local implementation of federal policy remains a key variable; a report from Wyoming notes a "limited rollout" of a new ICE partnership with its Highway Patrol, with a local advocate warning the agreement could "undermine public safety" [3].
