## 2. Foreign Policy and National Security

### 2.1. U.S.-Russia Relations and the War in Ukraine

In the aftermath of the high-profile Trump-Putin summit, the possibility of a negotiated settlement to the war in Ukraine is a primary subject of reporting and official commentary. Secretary of State Marco Rubio has publicly confirmed that a ceasefire deal is "not off the table" [1]. He further elaborated that any resolution will require significant concessions from "both sides," indicating that both Ukraine and Russia would need to make sacrifices to end the conflict [2]. A significant new element in these discussions is a claim from a Trump envoy that President Putin agreed in principle to allow both the U.S. and Europe to offer Ukraine "NATO-style" security protections or guarantees [3, 4]. This potential framework has spurred immediate analysis of its strategic consequences, with one outlet examining what "handing Donbas to Putin" would mean for Ukrainian sovereignty and morale [5]. The summit itself, however, has drawn sharp criticism from domestic political opponents and commentators, with some asserting that "Putin outplays Trump yet again" [6].
